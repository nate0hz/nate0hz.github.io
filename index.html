Interest in IT

My interest in IT ebbed and flowed in early life, but it began in my pre-teen years, with many weekends programming basic games on the family’s Commodore Vic-20. More substantial interest grew through high school as I discovered DOS but it was when I discovered some of the benefits that can come with networking and the internet, that my interest was truly sparked. Automation, machine learning and IOT are the areas that currently interest me the most.

As I finished year 12, I could not decide on an field of study clearly enough to justify spending 4 years studying. Computer Science was a clear option, as were Law and Business. At the time, my priority was leaving Tasmania, which I managed by enrolling in a Melbourne TAFE. The enrolment was an excuse and as I was more enthusiastic about discovering big city life than I was about the course, I predictably dropped out. I had always intended to study in earnest at RMIT, due to its reputation and quality, and after many years of excuses, I have finally committed to a few single units.

Initially I am completing a few introductory units as I want to get a feel for where technology is going. The emerging technologies and their applications are what interests me most, and introductory units tend to focus on wide and shallow coverage of many topics. I hope to test my preconceptions against the coverage of these fields, before settling on a field to pursue in depth.

Ideal job

I genuinely have my ideal job for right now. As Service Delivery Lead, I am able to combine my interest in technology, processes and well-honed understanding of user experience through the workings of the Service Desk. The entire point of this journey of learning, and possibly committing to a degree, is to seek out areas of interest for and to prepare for my next step.

While I expect this to change as my knowledge grows, something along the lines of Cloud Operations Director (Seek 2020) appears in line with my interests.

Remote work is being analysed by some organisations as a long term possibility (Ngyuen 2020) now that the onset of Coronavirus in 2020 has shown that it has not had a negative effect on productivity (Quiggin 2020) and that many workers desire the ability to work remotely for at least some of the time (Ziffer 2020). Even if remote working within Australia is not fully embraced, from my personal experience, I have witnessed some large multi-nationals embracing teams in various locations around the world providing services across the entire company, while others outsource international business partners to perform certain work, while wanting them to be well connected to the company. For this to be possible, Cloud solutions for application deployment are needed, such as Microsoft Azure, as are integrated communication and workspace tools, including solutions like Microsoft Teams, Sharepoint, Power BI, all of which are cloud based and able to help to analyse workflows and practices for improvement opportunities. Completing studies and working in a future focussed role or industry helps with future job security.

This type of role requires innovative approaches to solving problems, and afford the opportunity to implement a solution that can remove inefficiencies or “baked-in,” legacy process issues, which aligns with my interests.

As this is a senior position, leading implementing a score business strategy, well developed skills, experience and knowledge are critical. The advertisement describes a desire for 10 years or more experience in “progressively more experienced positions in Cloud Operations/DevOps roles” (Seek, 2020), indicating the organisation is seeking an individual who has developed over time. They also desire a person with “extensive experience” in AWS and/or Azure, along with other desirable traits and experience.

Currently I lack much of the experience required for the role. I satisfy, to a lesser degree, transferrable skills, like management and continuous improvement, but the technical knowledge and the implementation of the technical knowledge are areas I do not yet have experience in.

Learning key Azure skills can begin with Microsoft’s own online courses (Microsoft 2020), and furthered through professional courses, operated by local training organisations, such as DDLS (DDLS 2020). AWS offer similar training packages for students (AWS 2020). I believe the best path for me to move into a role like this is to leverage both the opportunities at my employer and my studies to assist the cloud solutions team, working on projects to further grow skills and knowledge. That experience can then be used to apply for dedicated project or architect roles and in turn demonstrate a history of “progressively more experienced positions.” (Seek 2020).

Project

My proposed project is to maintain a live database of items in my fridge, which can be accessed anywhere on my mobile, so I can see what I have and what I need, while at the supermarket. Favourite and essential items that are out of stock, or low in stock, will be flagged and listed first, so the user can easily determine what items need to be purchased.

The project uses a webcam, connected to a Raspberry Pi, to capture images of the items, and object recognition would interpret the image to add entries to or remove them from the database on purchase or use, respectively. The database would instantly upload the refreshed database to an almost live webpage.

The motivation here is that I am terrible at making shopping lists and even worse at remembering the items I need to buy when at the supermarket, which is evidenced by my cupboard holding five litres of shampoo, yet I can never remember to buy ginger. Clearly I am not alone here, as Samsung has released a Family Hub Fridge, which puts cameras inside the fridge, to give a live view of the fridge’s contents (Samsung 2020), and several enthusiastic Pi users have followed with their various versions (Horne 2016).

While many who have tackled this problem are aligned with Samsung’s thoughts of offering live or static images (mikeandlauren n.d), and one project suggested using load sensors to measure an item’s weight (aeckard87 2013), the proposed approach would overcome some problems with existing solutions, such as the camera’s restrictions of only being able to see the items at the front of shelves, or having to install and set up multiple, relatively expensive (Amazon n.d.) load sensors throughout the fridge.

The bill of materials for the solution is short, and would be built from items mostly already found at home, including:

  • A high definition web camera (already owned)
  • A Raspberry Pi 4B (already owned)
  • A monitor, for observing the object recognition success from tensor flow, during prototyping and could later be replaced with a mobile device (already owned)
  • Google TensorFlow, to allow for object recognition
  • Possibly software to scan and read barcodes for packaged items (several suggestions on the Raspberry Pi Forum)
  • An undetermined , simple database to store the inventory (several possibilities in IOT Stack)
  • Undetermined, simple web hosting software, so that the webpage can be hosted on the Pi (again, bundled in IOTStack)
  • A three or four button module, for user input and to change modes (something like this)
  • Python to write a simple program to detect and action the input from the button module
The current concept is that the device would be installed in the kitchen, with the camera pointing at an area on the bench. On returning home from grocery shopping, the user would place some items in the scanning space, wait for tensor flow to recognise the objects and quantities, then press a button to initiate the Python script and append the items to the inventory. When the user wishes to use an item from the fridge, they would place it in the scanning area, wait for TensorFlow to recognise the object and press another button, removing it from the inventory. As the inventory is adjusted, the database is uploaded to the hosted web page in real time.

Multiple new skills will be required for this project, including:
  • Enabling the web camera to function and capture imaged with the Raspberry Pi
  • Writing a TensorFlow model
  • Training the model to recognise select objects
  • Determining how to get the object recognised by TensorFlow into the database
  • Programming the buttons and their actions, and interfacing those with the other applications
  • Writing the code to enable a regularly refreshing web page, drawing data from a database
  • Installing and setting up web hosting software.
As I wrote the list I started to realise the scale of the new skills required for this project, and while feasible to create, due to time constraints, it is unlikely it will be completed within this course.

However, when it is completed, the project will enable my household to know exactly what is in the fridge at any time. This will greatly assist in efficient shopping, but can also assist with meal planning without needing to be physically in front of the fridge, when deciding what to cook with ingredients already in our possession.
